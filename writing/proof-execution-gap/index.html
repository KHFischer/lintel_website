The Proof-Execution Gap

Kalle H. Fischer — Founder, Lintel Standard · Published 2026-03-14

An allocator cannot verify what a systematic manager computed — only what they report

The due-diligence conversations I have had with systematic fund managers, and the ones I have reconstructed from allocator contacts, consistently terminate at the same wall. An allocator asks for the backtest to be re-run on a different window, or for the position time series underlying a stated Sharpe, or for the raw signal output on a specific date. The manager declines — not out of evasion, but because reproducing the backtest requires handing over the strategy itself, which is the fund’s only asset. Both positions are reasonable. The allocator’s job is to verify; the manager’s job is to protect. The tension is hard-coded into the industry’s own process artefacts: the AIMA Illustrative Due Diligence Questionnaire asks managers to describe signal generation, execution logic, and risk controls at a level of detail no manager will deliver on an unsigned NDA, and no NDA converts prose disclosure into verifiable computation. The result is a verification dead-end the industry has quietly agreed to call due diligence.

The pattern recurs across the systematic-manager conversations I have joined or reconstructed. It is not a function of any particular manager’s posture, nor of a particular allocator’s rigour, nor of the regulatory regime the two happen to be in. When the same pattern appears across counterparties with no shared motive, the pattern is structural rather than behavioural. That is the first signal worth naming.

Before going further, a disclosure the reader is entitled to up front: I am building in this space. Lintel is a company I founded to work on exactly the problem this article defines, and the commercial interest biases me toward believing the problem is real and worth solving. The article is published because I think the diagnosis stands independent of whether Lintel succeeds; the reader is invited to discount the framing accordingly.

The standard response to the dead-end is to layer more infrastructure on top of it. Fund administrators reconcile NAVs; risk analytics providers ingest position files; operational DD consultants interview staff and inspect controls. None of these layers touches the computation that actually produced the backtest; each sits downstream of the dead-end, not across it. The strongest practical objection to what follows is that this does not matter — that reputation, track record, admin reconciliations, and ODD interviews together produce a composite signal rich enough for real allocator decisions. I take the objection seriously and answer it in sections 2, 4, and 6; the short version is that the composite works at the top of the manager distribution and fails predictably at the bottom, and the failure mode is architectural rather than procedural. The next section gives the structural pattern a name. I call it the Proof-Execution Gap.

The Proof-Execution Gap names an architectural failure, not a behavioural one

The Proof-Execution Gap is the structural inability to bind a claim about a computation to the computation that produced it. When a manager reports that a particular strategy, run on a particular data set, produced a particular return series, the allocator has no mechanism by which to confirm that the computation described is the computation that occurred. The missing primitive is claim-to-computation binding. Without it, a self-attested claim about a computation is falsifiable only against other artefacts produced by the same party — the parametric equivalent of checking a signature by asking the signer to re-write it.

To make the mechanism concrete, consider a manager reporting a 2.1 Sharpe backtest on EUR/USD from 2015 through 2024. Before claim-to-computation binding, the allocator has a factsheet, a return series, and a prose strategy description. Those artefacts verify that the manager has produced certain files containing certain numbers; the allocator can check internal consistency and compare the return series against the stated exposure, but no check answers whether the series was produced by the strategy described or by any other process whatsoever. After binding, the same return series arrives with a hardware-signed attestation stating that a binary identified by hash B, against a data reference identified by hash D, produced exactly that series. The first artefact is falsifiable against nothing but itself. The second is falsifiable against the binary and the data without the allocator ever seeing either. That is the mechanism; the rest of the article is about why the difference is structurally decisive rather than incremental.

The gap is a category property, not a quality property. It does not narrow as a manager discloses more. A hundred-page DDQ response describing the strategy in prose, accompanied by a factsheet, a risk report, and a letter from a respected PM, contains the same class of verifiable information about the underlying computation as a single-sentence claim: self-attested information from the manager, with no independent attestation channel beneath it. The additional pages raise the cost of fabrication and improve internal-consistency checks; they do not introduce a verification primitive the earlier artefacts lacked. The distinction is the epistemic difference between a single witness repeating a story and a second independent witness corroborating it.

The strongest practical-sufficiency objection is that repeated disclosure over time — monthly factsheets, fund-admin-struck NAVs, tax filings, regulator submissions, broker statements — produces enough correlated self-attestation that the composite is good enough for real allocator decisions. The objection is correct at the top of the manager distribution, where a decade of admin-reconciled NAVs, audited financials, and reputational collateral makes consistent fabrication expensive. What the composite is not: an independent verification channel beneath the self-attestation layer. It is a set of mutually-dependent self-attestations whose cost of consistent fabrication happens to be high in that specific regime, and which fails where it is most needed — at emerging managers with short track records, light admin history, and no reputational collateral.

I am naming something specific, and it helps to name what it is not. The Proof-Execution Gap is not fraud detection. Fraud detection presumes the question of whether the manager is lying is the interesting question; the gap holds whether or not the manager is honest, because an honest self-attestation is epistemically indistinguishable from a dishonest one in the absence of binding. It is not operational due diligence. Operational DD asks whether the manager has the controls, staff, and governance to run a fund; the gap sits one layer below, at the computation itself. It is not model risk management. Model risk asks whether the strategy is likely to fail in a given regime; the gap asks whether the strategy the allocator has been shown is the strategy in question at all.

The gap is also scale-independent. A multi-billion-AUM shop produces self-attested computational claims with the same structural non-verifiability as a three-month-old CTA. The architectures that close the gap are not reserved for any particular tier of manager, because the gap is not a function of size. With the category named and bounded, I can now turn to why the pattern is recognisable.

The pre-1933 state of financial reporting is the direct structural analogue

American financial reporting before 1933 sat in a structurally similar position to systematic-fund claims today: issuers published financial statements, investors had no mechanism by which to verify them independently, and the absence of a verification mechanism was not remedied by more or better disclosure. Companies filed earnings; investors took the filings on faith, on reputation, or not at all. The disclosure regime existed. The verification regime did not. The parallel is precise on one dimension — self-attested claims flowing to capital allocators without an independent attestation channel — and explicitly not a parallel on institutional form: the 1933 regime addressed public issuers filing statutory disclosures, while the systematic-manager diligence regime is private, bilateral, and contractual. The narrow claim I defend is structural, not institutional.

The resolution was legislative but not primarily about disclosure. The Pujo Committee, convened in 1912 and reporting in 1913, documented the concentration of capital markets power under a small group of financiers who controlled information flow as much as capital flow. Two decades later, the Securities Act of 1933, Section 11 and the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, Section 17 responded. Both acts required disclosure, but the load-bearing move in each was the creation of mandatory independent audit: a chartered profession of parties whose function was to attest that what the issuer reported was what the issuer’s books contained. The Acts did not demand more detailed issuer self-reporting. They created a new category of actor standing between the issuer and the capital markets — and, importantly, that actor attested to the accounting identity of reported figures against books, not to the economic substance of the business itself. Stephen Zeff (2003) has traced how this specific delineation, between attesting to the books and attesting to the enterprise, shaped the profession for the next nine decades.

The certified public accountant profession that emerged was new infrastructure, not a rebranding of existing actors. The AICPA and its state-level antecedents were organising a new intermediary class, with admission standards, a body of technical doctrine, and legal privileges tied to the attestation function (Previts & Merino, A History of Accountancy in the United States). This matters because the analogy here is specifically about category creation rather than process improvement. The pre-1933 problem was not solved by better disclosure forms, a larger SEC, or more sophisticated investors. It was solved by instantiating a category of verification actor that had not previously existed.

I draw the analogy carefully and defend only a narrow version of it. The strongest objection is institutional, not technical: statutory issuer reporting and private-manager diligence are structurally different systems — the former compulsory, standardised, and backed by securities law; the latter voluntary, bilateral, and governed by fund documents and NDAs. No legislative template ports cleanly across the two, and I am not calling for the SEC to charter a profession of computation auditors. The narrower claim the analogy does support is this: when self-attested claims flow to capital allocators without an independent attestation channel, the historically effective response has been to instantiate a new category of verification actor rather than to refine the self-attestation format. In 2026 that category will most likely be produced technically and contractually rather than statutorily; that is a judgment, not a prediction the analogy forces. The analogy is a frame for recognising the shape of the problem and the categorical nature of the historical resolution. That frame is what I need for the next section, which asks which layer in the current systematic-finance stack is positioned to produce the needed category, and answers: none of them.

Current DD infrastructure operates downstream of the gap, not across it

Fund administrators, risk analytics providers, and operational due-diligence consultants each perform valuable work on manager-reported data. None of them verifies the computation that produced the data. Each sits at a layer in the stack positioned downstream of the Proof-Execution Gap, not across it. Walking through the stack makes the architectural point concrete.

Fund administrators strike NAVs by reconciling positions the manager reports to the positions the prime broker or custodian records on their books (Citco Fund Administration & Middle Office Solutions). That reconciliation is a genuine verification function, and it catches a meaningful class of errors: accounting miscounts, missed corporate actions, stale prices. It does not, and is not intended to, verify that the strategy the manager describes is the strategy whose trades the prime broker recorded. Published service-level agreements at major administrators scope the reconciliation to prime-broker-recorded positions against manager-reported ones — not to strategy identity (SS&C Shadow Administration and Reconciliation Services). A manager reporting a systematic-momentum position to the fund admin, with the underlying book populated by discretionary bets that happen to net to the reported exposure, is reconciled cleanly. The administrator confirms accounting identity, not computational identity.

Risk analytics providers — MSCI Barra, Bloomberg PORT, Axioma, and peers, listed here illustratively rather than as teardowns — ingest manager-supplied returns and position data and decompose it against factor models. The analysis is sophisticated. The inputs are self-attested. The output is a factor decomposition of what the manager said they did, which is a distinct object from a factor decomposition of what the manager actually did. A sophisticated model downstream does not repair the absence of binding upstream.

Operational due-diligence consultants sell human judgment on controls, governance, staff, and infrastructure, of which AIMA’s DDQ framework is the canonical template. ODD work is legitimate and often high-value, and I am not proposing it is redundant. It is orthogonal to computation verification. A fund can have exemplary controls, a tenured team, and a clean regulatory record, and the Proof-Execution Gap is exactly as wide at that fund as at a three-month-old CTA running out of a garage.

The strongest counter-argument deserves a direct engagement, because it is correct. The existing due-diligence stack has intermediated a large share of the institutional systematic-fund market — low single-digit trillions of dollars per HFR’s global hedge fund AUM estimates, of which systematic strategies are a material share (HFR Global Hedge Fund Industry Report) — without cryptographic attestation, and it is not obviously broken. At the top tier of manager — established firms with institutional allocators, long track records, and the staff to field sophisticated DD — the stack works well enough that the case for new verification infrastructure looks like a solution in search of a problem. I concede this. The stack’s failure is located specifically at the emerging-manager margin, which industry surveys consistently locate as a small share of allocated capital relative to its share of manager count (Preqin Global Report: Hedge Funds). This is the segment where self-attestation signal-to-noise is lowest, where the allocator cannot reliably distinguish a good emerging manager from a bad one on reputation alone, and where the gap determines who receives capital and who does not. The failure is architectural because no combination of the existing layers produces a primitive that binds a claim about a computation to a sealed execution of specified code. Reconciliation does not do it. Factor analysis does not do it. Human judgment on controls does not do it. The needed primitive is not in the stack.

That absence is the specification for what must be built, which is the question the next section takes up.

Closing the gap requires sealed execution, content-addressed strategy identity, and hardware attestation

An architecture that closes the Proof-Execution Gap needs three primitives, and describing them at the architectural level is the useful exercise — the one that survives whichever vendor implements them. The three are sealed execution, content-addressed strategy identity, and hardware attestation.

Sealed execution is the property that the strategy runs inside an environment the manager cannot alter mid-computation, and whose state is attestable by a party that is neither the manager nor me. In practice this is a Trusted Execution Environment: a hardware-isolated region of memory and CPU state protected from the host operating system, the hypervisor, and the cloud provider, as defined in the CCC’s “A Technical Analysis of Confidential Computing”. The manager can deposit code into the enclave; the enclave executes the code; the host can starve the enclave of resources but cannot read or modify its protected state. The primitive the enclave provides to the verification problem is a computation whose execution integrity does not depend on the honesty of the party who owns the machine.

Content-addressed strategy identity is the property that a strategy is identified by the hash of the compiled artifact, not by a name the manager has assigned. “Strategy X” resolves to one specific binary, and any change to the code, the configuration, or any dependency produces a different binary and a different identity. This matters because the Proof-Execution Gap is, at core, an identity problem: the allocator needs to be able to refer to the same computation the manager is describing, and a name assigned by the manager is not an identity in the technical sense. A hash is.

Hardware attestation is the property that the hardware vendor — Intel, AMD, or another silicon manufacturer with a root of trust embedded in the CPU — signs a report binding four specific artefacts: the hash of the compiled strategy binary, the measured state of the enclave environment (microcode version, firmware, loaded runtime libraries), a hash reference to the input data used, and the hash of the output the enclave produced. The allocator verifies the vendor’s signature against a public vendor key, without seeing the strategy code itself. Binding those four artefacts together is what converts “a claim about a computation” into “a verifiable proof that a specific computation, against specific inputs, produced a specific output.” Vendor neutrality is architectural rather than accidental; more than one silicon manufacturer produces TEE-capable hardware, and the architecture generalises across them.

The honest concession: TEEs have documented vulnerabilities, and I will not sell the primitives as trust-eliminating. Foreshadow (CVE-2018-3615), SGAxe (CVE-2020-0549), Plundervolt (Murdock et al. 2020, CVE-2019-11157), and CacheWarp (CVE-2023-20592) are four examples in a lengthening catalogue of side-channel and fault-injection attacks on commercial TEE implementations. Attestation does not eliminate trust; it shifts trust from the manager to the hardware vendor and the patch cadence of the enclave implementation. Revocation and patching are therefore part of the model rather than afterthoughts: vendors publish CVEs and issue microcode patches, and a verifier that honours the published disclosures treats attestations produced under compromised microcode versions as failing verification once the vulnerability is known. The defensible framing is defense-in-depth: sealed execution is one layer among several, and the architecture worth building treats TEEs as a strong-but-not-sole basis for attestation, with observable compensating layers around it.

The alternatives deserve engagement on their merits. Zero-knowledge proofs, secure multi-party computation, and fully homomorphic encryption address adjacent versions of the same problem, and each is a live field of research (arXiv: A Survey on the Applications of Zero-Knowledge Proofs). ZK proofs give cryptographic guarantees without hardware trust assumptions but currently carry prover-side computational costs that make them unworkable for the scale and cadence of systematic strategy backtesting; published benchmarks on workloads comparable to a multi-year multi-asset backtest sit orders of magnitude above the compute budget an emerging manager can carry per allocator conversation (ZKML: An Optimizing System for ML Inference in Zero-Knowledge Proofs). MPC and FHE carry their own trade-offs around liveness, participant count, and performance. Confidential-computing VMs, by contrast, are now general-availability cloud offerings on Azure, AWS, and GCP at a modest premium above commodity compute, as documented in Microsoft’s Azure confidential VM overview. My judgment — held as a judgment, not as a fact claim — is that TEE-anchored attestation is the approach with working deployment economics at systematic-fund workload sizes in 2026. It is not the only conceptually valid path, and it is not the end state. It is what closes the gap with today’s hardware.

Closing the gap compresses sales cycles, adds a verifiable DDQ-time filter, and unclogs the emerging-manager gate

If the Proof-Execution Gap closes, the primary economic effect is a reduction in screening cost at the emerging-manager margin. The sales-cycle compression and DDQ-filter consequences I describe below are subordinate effects of that central change, not independent benefits, and I frame them that way.

Consider the concrete before-and-after for an allocator evaluating an emerging manager whose stated strategy is systematic multi-asset trend-following. Before attestation, the diligence packet contains: a strategy description in prose, a factsheet, a return series, reference checks on the PM and team, a fund-admin-verified NAV history where one exists, and an ODD interview. The allocator decides on the composite of these signals and accepts residual uncertainty about whether the described strategy generated the described returns. After attestation, the packet contains the same artefacts plus a hardware-signed attestation binding the return series to a specific binary hash and a specific data reference. The qualitative judgment — is this a good team, is this a good strategy, does this fit the mandate — remains entirely human. What changes is that the specific question “is the backtest produced by the strategy the team describes” becomes pass-fail and can be answered without an IP exchange. That is the screening-cost reduction; the effects below follow from it.

On the manager side, the verification artefact is produced once and shown many times. A manager who compiles an attested package pays the compilation cost once, and IP-exposure risk per pitch drops because the package is demonstrable without handing over the strategy itself. I will not claim a specific industry-wide figure for today’s cost per emerging-manager meeting; I will claim that compiled attestation shifts the variable-cost component of that cost from “per meeting” toward “per compile,” and the two cost curves do not scale the same way, an economic bottleneck documented by recent AIMA research highlighting that emerging-manager sales cycles now average eight months of continuous, variable-cost due diligence per ticket.

On the allocator side, the verifiability check slots into the existing DDQ as a pass-fail artefact at a specific point in the diligence flow — concretely, as an item alongside admin confirmations rather than alongside manager-authored representations. Human judgment on strategy, team, fees, and drawdown profile is unchanged; what changes is that the specific backtest-to-strategy binding stops being taken on faith.

The strongest adoption objection is operational, not conceptual. An allocator’s operational due-diligence team will not absorb a new binary check without asking the questions they ask of any vendor: who owns the control, what happens when the vendor is acquired or fails, what is the exception-handling policy when attestation fails for non-malicious reasons — a microcode patch mid-backtest, a hardware revocation event, a cloud-provider outage — and how does the check integrate with the existing DDQ artefact set and the allocator’s own vendor-diligence framework. These are the right questions. The adoption path for computation verification runs through procurement language — control ownership, vendor diligence, business continuity, exception handling — as much as through cryptographic argument. A vendor in this space that cannot answer the procurement questions will not cross the emerging-manager procurement gate regardless of the elegance of its underlying primitive, and the work of translating attestation semantics into ODD-legible controls is part of the specification rather than an afterthought.

The concrete audience action follows. An allocator reading this can ask a specific question on their next manager call: can you produce a cryptographic attestation that the backtest I am looking at was produced by the strategy you are describing, running on the data you are describing. The answer is diagnostic regardless of which vendor, if any, provides the attestation. A manager who can produce one, and a manager who cannot, are in distinguishable positions. The question is the filter.

I am building in this space; the piece stands without us

I am building in this space; the disclosure in section 1 stands, and this coda adds what that disclosure did not: a falsifiability test and a specification of what any credible solution would have to contain. The test: if three years from now the emerging-manager allocation gate still turns on IP-exposure risk rather than strategy quality, the Proof-Execution Gap framing has failed as a predictive claim and should be discarded as founder theorising that did not survive market behaviour. The specification: any implementation of the argument — Lintel’s or otherwise — needs sealed execution, content-addressed strategy identity, hardware attestation, and a verifier standing outside both the manager and the vendor, or it is not an implementation of the argument. My formal specifications of the Open Veracity Language (SSRN 6388459) (the declarative language for expressing strategy computations for enclaved execution) and the Proof-Execution Gap (SSRN 6090766) (the attestation and verification flow) are on SSRN for anyone who wants to engage the technical argument directly. The Proof-Execution Gap is the argument; Lintel is one implementation of it, and the filter for either is whether allocators start asking the diagnostic question.

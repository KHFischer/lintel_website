Defense in Depth for Attested Execution

Kalle H. Fischer — Founder, Lintel Standard · Published 2026-04-25

The right question is what survives a TEE break, not whether TEEs can break

Trusted Execution Environments have a published record of serious, remotely exploitable vulnerabilities — Foreshadow / L1TF (Van Bulck et al., USENIX Security 2018), Plundervolt (Murdock et al., IEEE S&P 2020), SGAxe (Van Schaik et al., 2020), CacheWarp (CVE-2023-20592, USENIX Security 2024), BadRAM (KU Leuven, 2024). That is two CPU vendors and seven years of public disclosure. Any architecture that treats a TEE as a single point of trust has repeatedly failed under those CVEs. If you are reading this as a security researcher, you have probably written one of those papers.

Lintel builds cryptographic strategy verification infrastructure and our product uses TEEs. The argument here is about architecture, not Lintel. Judge it on its merits.

The strongest objection to what I am about to argue is direct: remote attestation is the root runtime binding for a Strategy Verification Package (SVP). If a full TEE compromise is possible — and the CVE record above says it is — then every downstream proof derived from that attestation should collapse with it. On its own terms, the objection holds. A proof whose only binding to reality is one hardware signature inherits the fragility of that signature. The claim of this piece is narrower: that TEEs are fragile and TEEs are a load-bearing security primitive are compatible, provided the architecture is designed so that a complete TEE compromise degrades rather than collapses the trust model. I will call the weaker posture TEE-reliant and the stronger posture TEE-inclusive. The distinction is falsifiable. In a TEE-reliant design, a single Foreshadow-class CVE lets an attacker forge a valid proof. In a TEE-inclusive design, the same CVE costs one specific layer of guarantees, and the proof fails verification at every other layer.

Concretely: an attacker with a Foreshadow-class leak of SGX attestation keys can mint arbitrary attestation quotes, but cannot mint them over the content-addressed identity a verifier has already pinned — the identity is a SHA-256 hash of the strategy specification, not a secret the enclave holds. The forged bundle fails at the identity check before any attestation signature is consulted. That is the shape of a cross-layer failure: one layer broken, the proof still rejected.

Defense in depth, in the SVP sense, is five independent layers, each binding the claim to the computation through a different mechanism — content-addressed identity, sub-Turing language semantics, TEE hardware attestation, a public transparency log, and a bundle-level commitment signature, as set out in the CVP protocol specification (SSRN 6090766). The rest of this piece walks through each, names what it preserves when the neighbouring layer breaks, and maps each named CVE onto the layers it does and does not affect.

Strategy identity is the hash of the specification, not the output of the enclave

Layer 1 is content-addressed identity. A strategy’s identity in the SVP is the SHA-256 Merkle root of its canonical specification — the CVP paper (SSRN 6090766, §4.2) calls this the CVP_ID. The specification has four components: Logic (L, the executable container), Data Manifest (D, the canonical list of content-addressed input pointers), Environment Specification (E, the required TEE parameters), and Test Specification (T, the standardised disclosure harness). Each component is hashed independently and the leaves are composed into a root: h_LD = H(h_L || h_D), h_ET = H(h_E || h_T), CVP_ID = H(h_LD || h_ET). Canonicalisation before hashing follows the RFC 8785 JSON Canonicalization Scheme, which makes the hash stable across independent implementations.

The layer protects a verifier who pins the expected CVP_ID before checking anything else. A reader who accepts whatever CVP_ID an attacker hands them has no protection from this layer at all — they are verifying a different strategy, and the identity binding is doing no work. The verifier either holds the expected CVP_ID from a prior registration or recomputes it from the canonical spec components (L, D, E, T) published by the prover. The first step of the CVP verifier algorithm (SSRN 6090766, §9.2, step 1) is exactly this: reconstruct the Merkle root from the provided component commitments and assert that it matches the claimed CVP_ID. Any mismatch terminates verification in INVALID before any attestation or signature check runs.

The consequence: a strategy’s identity is the hash of what the strategy is, not the output of the machine that ran it. Modifying a single byte of L, D, E, or T produces a different CVP_ID. An attacker who fully owns the TEE in 2028 — who can mint any attestation they like — still mints it over a different CVP_ID than the one the verifier pinned. Because step 1 of the verifier rejects any bundle whose reconstructed root does not equal the pinned CVP_ID, the forged bundle fails at the identity check and no attestation signature is ever consulted. Independent verifiers reject the forgery as a different strategy, not as a compromised result of the strategy they care about.

The binding is cryptographic and external to the runtime. A TEE break in 2028 cannot retroactively mutate a CVP_ID issued in 2026, because the identity does not depend on the TEE having ever run.

Sub-Turing semantics bound behaviour statically, independent of any runtime

Layer 2 is language-level. Logic in the SVP is expressed in the Open Veracity Language (OVL), whose computational model is a finite acyclic dataflow graph of total functions over typed domains, as defined in the OVL specification (SSRN 6388459, §4). Sub-Turing here has a specific meaning: no loops with data-dependent termination, no recursion, no mutable state, no cycles. The language sits strictly below synchronous dataflow and below total functional programming in the computational hierarchy (OVL specification, SSRN 6388459, §4, Fig. 1; Turner, “Total Functional Programming,” 2004). Termination, determinism, and bounded resource consumption follow from the computational model itself, not from runtime enforcement.

The counter-argument is real and needs answering before anything else: a language restriction does not prove execution honesty on a compromised host. A compromised OVL runtime can emit any bytes it wants. Sub-Turing semantics do not stop a dishonest runtime from lying. What they provide is checkability. Because the spec defines a deterministic, total function from inputs to output, any independent party holding the canonical inputs can re-execute the spec under a known-good runtime and compare bit-for-bit. A compromised runtime for a Turing-complete program has no such check available — the same program can produce different outputs legitimately (non-determinism, I/O, state), and static reasoning cannot separate deviation from legitimate variation. For an OVL program the deviation signal is unambiguous: same L, same D, same E, different O means either the runtime is lying or the canonical inputs disagree, and both conditions are testable.

The downstream verification mechanism is specific. An independent verifier — an allocator’s ODD consultant, a regulator, Lintel running a reference implementation, or any party with access to D — fetches the canonical Data Manifest D (whose content-addressed pointers make it reproducible), runs the OVL program defined by L on D inside a reference executor committing to E, and compares the output hash to the O in the SVP bundle. Mismatch fails verification at Layer 2 because the two outputs are bit-identical under a total deterministic function or they are not (OVL specification, SSRN 6388459, §4 on determinism; CVP protocol specification, SSRN 6090766, §5.1 on input consistency check). Without the sub-Turing restriction, “rerun and compare” is not a valid check — the output space is not a function of the inputs alone. With it, the comparison is mechanical.

Layer 1 binds the spec to an identity. Layer 2 binds the spec to a behaviour that is externally checkable by anyone who holds the inputs. Both bindings hold without the verifier trusting the original runtime, which is what makes them independent of the TEE.

An allocator or ODD reviewer can exercise this layer directly: request the Data Manifest D from the manager, rerun L against D under a reference OVL executor, and compare the output hash to the SVP. Any bit-level mismatch is a Layer 2 failure, independent of whether the original enclave was ever compromised.

A TEE attests the binary and the platform, not the correctness of the computation

Layer 3 is where the Trusted Execution Environment lives. Remote attestation, which is what the layer produces, is a signed statement from a hardware root of trust asserting three things: which binary was loaded, which platform loaded it, and which firmware version the platform was running. For AMD SEV-SNP, the signing chain is AMD Root Key (ARK) → AMD Signing Key (ASK) → Versioned Chip Endorsement Key (VCEK), terminating in a quote signed by the specific CPU on which the enclave executed, as documented in the AMD SEV-SNP Firmware ABI Specification (Pub. 56860). Intel TDX uses a structurally similar chain terminating in the platform quoting enclave, as described in the Intel TDX attestation documentation.

The Trusted Computing Base (TCB) is the set of hardware and firmware components whose correctness the attestation assumes. The SVP enforces a minimum Security Version Number (SVN) policy: attestations produced under firmware older than the declared minimum are rejected at verification time (CVP protocol specification, SSRN 6090766, §7.1). The CVP paper calls this Patch or Perish. It is the operational policy that keeps a TEE’s attestation meaningful after a CVE is disclosed and patched.

What the layer buys: code-measurement binding (this exact binary ran), nonce freshness (this attestation is not a replay), and platform state under a declared TCB. What it does not buy: semantic correctness of the computation, and immunity to side-channel attacks not yet published, a boundary made explicit in the CCC’s “Technical Analysis of Confidential Computing” (v1.3).

The obvious objection: if attestation does not prove correctness, why keep Layer 3 at all — why not rely on Layer 2’s independent re-execution? The answer is that Layer 3 is what makes re-execution a fallback rather than a default. Attestation gives a verifier a cheap pre-filter: a valid quote under a current TCB SVN means the binary that produced O was the one the spec claims, running on a platform whose TCB the vendor has not flagged. A verifier who trusts the quote can skip re-execution and accept the result. A verifier who does not trust it can always fall back to Layer 2. Without attestation, every SVP would require independent re-execution to be meaningful, which is economically untenable at the volumes institutional allocators care about. Layer 3 buys a default-accept path under a declared TCB and a default-reject path once Patch or Perish flags the SVN as stale.

Foreshadow, SGAxe, Plundervolt, CacheWarp, and BadRAM are contributions to the field. The architecture treats them as expected Layer 3 failures and limits their blast radius through the other four layers: a published CVE triggers a TCB SVN bump, which mechanically rejects attestations chaining through the vulnerable firmware at verification time, while Layers 1, 2, 4, and 5 remain undisturbed by the attestation compromise.

An allocator or ODD reviewer can require, in writing, the TCB SVN each SVP was issued under and the minimum SVN the verifier enforces; any SVP attested below the declared minimum fails Layer 3 regardless of who ran it.

A public transparency log makes silent retraction of an SVP detectable

Layer 4 makes silent retraction of an SVP detectable. The mechanism is the SVP Transparency Log — an append-only Merkle tree that records every SVP issuance as a leaf, with periodic signed tree heads published for external observers (CVP protocol specification, SSRN 6090766, §8, Fig. 8). The cryptographic primitive is borrowed directly from Certificate Transparency (RFC 6962, Laurie, Langley, Kasper, IETF 2013).

The log defends against silent retraction on a specific, operational condition: once an inclusion proof or mirror copy of a tree head exists outside the log operator’s control, later omission of that leaf is publicly demonstrable by comparison against the current log state. Before that point, the protection does not exist. A vendor that issues an SVP but has not yet published a signed tree head containing it, or has published a head that no independent party has retained, can still retract without evidence. The detection guarantee is a property of the gossip history, not of the log alone.

The social layer matters as much as the cryptography. A log run by one commercial vendor is a single point of trust, which is what defense in depth is supposed to dissolve. The correct architecture is multi-party mirrors run by independent auditors, consortia, and customers, plus verifiers that periodically gossip signed tree heads with each other. That protection is only as good as the gossip schedule: if independent monitors do not actually exchange signed tree heads on a recurring basis, detection is theoretical rather than operational. The residual threat is a split-view or equivocation attack, in which the operator presents inconsistent tree states to different observers (Chuat et al., “Efficient Gossip Protocols for Verifying the Consistency of Certificate Logs,” IEEE CNS 2015). Gossip makes split views detectable after the fact, provided monitors actually gossip.

An attacker who compromises the TEE and mints a fraudulent SVP is still observable in the SVP Transparency Log to any party who has retained an inclusion proof or a mirror copy. That is the precise condition under which silent retraction becomes provable.

An allocator or ODD reviewer can ask for the SVP’s inclusion proof at issuance time, retain it independently, and periodically fetch the current signed tree head from a mirror; any later omission is demonstrable by comparison.

The SVP commitment is a signature over the composed bundle, not the TEE’s attestation key

Layer 5 is a signature. The final artifact an allocator verifies is an SVP commitment: a digital signature over the composed bundle of (a) the CVP_ID, (b) the TEE attestation quote, © the oracle signatures on the input data manifest, and (d) the output hash (CVP protocol specification, SSRN 6090766, §4.3, Eq. 4). In the current protocol, the commitment is an ECDSA signature (CVP protocol specification, SSRN 6090766, §3 cryptographic assumptions) computed with a signing key SK_enc generated fresh inside the enclave at the start of each execution; the corresponding PK_enc is cryptographically bound to the attestation by including H(PK_enc) in the REPORT_DATA (SEV-SNP) or UserData (SGX) field of the hardware quote (CVP protocol specification, SSRN 6090766, §7.3).

The honest description of the current key custody path is therefore: SK_enc lives in enclave memory for the duration of an execution and is discarded. There is no separate HSM, threshold signer, or external KMS in the deployed protocol today. The strongest objection to calling this “Layer 5 separation” is direct: if SK_enc is generated inside the enclave and used inside it, then a live Layer 3 compromise that reads enclave memory — a Foreshadow-class leak on unpatched firmware, for example — can also leak SK_enc and forge a Layer 5 commitment for the currently running execution. I am not going to pretend otherwise. Moving SK_enc into independent custody (HSM-held signing key, threshold signing across an operator committee, or an external KMS) is target architecture, not current deployment; I flag this explicitly because conflating the two would be the most dangerous kind of architectural overclaim.

What the current Layer 5 design does provide is narrower but still load-bearing. Per-execution key freshness: SK_enc is new for every SVP, bound to that SVP’s specific attestation quote and TCB SVN, so an attacker who later compromises enclave memory under a newer TCB cannot retroactively forge past SVPs — each historical signature was produced under a different ephemeral key bound to a different quote. Nonce binding: the attestation request includes a verifier-chosen nonce, so replay of a captured signature under a different verifier fails (CVP protocol specification, SSRN 6090766, §7.3 challenge-response). Bundle-level integrity: the commitment signs the composed bundle, so tampering with any single component — the CVP_ID, the attestation, an oracle signature, the output hash — invalidates the signature. A partial compromise yields no valid SVP, only an invalid one (CVP protocol specification, SSRN 6090766, §11 residual-risk table).

Because the commitment signs the bundle, verification does not require live access to the TEE. An independent verifier with only the bundle and the attestation-bound PK_enc reaches a yes-or-no judgement. Once independent key custody is in place — a target I commit to here as a material protocol upgrade, not a marketing claim — the forge requirement at Layer 5 becomes “break ECDSA or exfiltrate a key the TEE compromise did not touch,” and the co-compromise concession narrows. Until then, the architecture’s separation is between historical and live SVPs, not between the commitment key and the attestation key.

An allocator or ODD reviewer can check the binding directly: verify that the REPORT_DATA or UserData field of the attestation quote equals H(PK_enc) for the PK_enc used to sign the SVP, and verify the ECDSA signature over the bundle offline from the supplied artifacts.

A Foreshadow-class break against Layer 3 degrades the TEE boundary without forging the SVP

This is the concrete test of the architecture. I walk through four published TEE CVEs and, for each, name what the attacker gains at Layer 3 and what they do not gain at Layers 1, 2, 4, and 5. The structure is the Gain / Does not gain by layer analysis frame, applied repeatedly. If a CVE cuts across multiple layers, the architecture fails; if each is confined to Layer 3, it holds.

One clarification up front on how historical artifacts are treated. When a CVE leaks attestation keys or enclave memory on a given TCB SVN, historical SVPs issued under that SVN are handled by Patch or Perish: verifiers applying the minimum-SVN policy reject those attestations outright at verification time, with an advisory marker appended to the affected leaves in the SVP Transparency Log (CVP protocol specification, SSRN 6090766, §7.1, §8). The architecture does not rehabilitate past artifacts under compromised firmware; it rejects them. Live SVPs on the compromised SVN are rejected on the same policy. Only SVPs attested under patched firmware are accepted going forward.

Foreshadow / L1TF (CVE-2018-3615) (Van Bulck et al., “Foreshadow: Extracting the Keys to the Intel SGX Kingdom,” USENIX Security 2018). A speculative-execution side channel that reads data from the L1 cache of a co-tenant enclave, including attestation keys on older Intel SGX platforms. Gains: confidentiality of enclave memory under specific microcode versions, including potential leakage of the ephemeral SK_enc for an execution running at the time of attack. Does not gain: the ability to modify the CVP_ID of a pinned strategy (Layer 1 is a hash of the spec, not a secret leaked from memory), to force agreement between the spec-defined function and an independent re-execution on patched hardware against the canonical D (Layer 2), to silently retract a prior log entry (Layer 4 is append-only and mirror-observable), or to forge historical SVPs whose per-execution ephemeral keys were bound to different, no-longer-compromised attestation quotes (Layer 5’s per-execution key freshness). Pre-patch SVPs are rejected by Patch or Perish at verification time regardless of whether their keys leaked.

Plundervolt (CVE-2019-11157) (Murdock et al., “Plundervolt: Software-based Fault Injection Attacks against Intel SGX,” IEEE S&P 2020). Software-controlled CPU undervolting that injects rare arithmetic faults inside SGX. **Gains:**the ability to corrupt a specific computation’s intermediate state and produce a perturbed output. Does not gain: a valid attestation over a modified binary, because attestation binds to the original code measurement, not to a perturbed execution. Provided an independent party holds the canonical Data Manifest D and a reference OVL executor committing to the same E on patched hardware, re-executing L against D yields a different result bit-for-bit — the observable-divergence signal Layer 2 is designed to produce. Without those preconditions (access to D, a reference executor), the divergence check is unavailable.

CacheWarp (CVE-2023-20592) (Zhang et al., “CacheWarp: Software-based Fault Injection using Selective State Reset,” USENIX Security 2024). An AMD SEV-SNP vulnerability permitting architectural rollback of cache lines, enabling control-flow manipulation inside the confidential VM. Gains: control-flow hijacking within an enclave running on unpatched SEV-SNP firmware. Does not gain: anything against an SVP whose attestation quote carries a TCB SVN equal to or greater than the minimum declared in E. Patch or Perish in Layer 3 mechanically rejects pre-patch attestations at verification time; SVPs issued under vulnerable firmware are not accepted, and SVPs issued under patched firmware are not exposed (AMD Security Bulletin AMD-SB-3005).

BadRAM (2024) (BadRAM: Practical Memory Aliasing Attacks on Trusted Execution Environments, KU Leuven 2024). Memory-integrity undermining on specific DRAM configurations by manipulating SPD data the memory controller reads at boot, affecting SEV-SNP’s memory encryption under particular modules. Gains: integrity violations of the confidential VM’s memory under specific hardware conditions, including potential read/write access to enclave-resident secrets such as SK_enc for a live execution. Does not gain: the content-addressed identity of a pinned strategy (Layer 1 is external to memory), the log inclusion proof for a prior SVP (Layer 4 is a cryptographic Merkle proof held by mirrors, not enclave-resident state), or historical SVPs attested under unaffected hardware configurations (Layer 5’s per-execution key freshness). Patch or Perish rejects SVPs attested under the affected SPD configurations once the CVE’s SVN range is declared.

The invariant across all four is the same. Each CVE affects the runtime binding between code and platform, and only that binding. None affects whether the code is what the spec says (Layer 1), whether the language can produce a result outside the function’s deterministic range when checked by an independent re-executor (Layer 2), whether history can be silently rewritten (Layer 4), or whether historical SVPs under unaffected TCB SVNs can be forged (Layer 5’s per-execution key freshness). Live SVPs on the compromised SVN are rejected under Patch or Perish at verification time.

A test-at-home for any reader. When the next TEE CVE is disclosed, map it onto the five layers. If the CVE touches only Layer 3, the architecture degrades under Patch or Perish and recovers with re-attestation on patched firmware. If a single CVE spans multiple layers — if it lets an attacker modify the content-addressed identity, break the signature scheme itself, or silently retract log entries — the architecture needs revision. That is a falsifiable claim. I want to be told about it when it fails.

A 72-hour TCB advisory protocol converts hardware CVEs into a procurement-legible incident response

Architecture is not operational until the vendor commits to the process that runs it. The SVP platform’s operational control here is a TCB advisory protocol with a 72-hour notification window, codified as a contractual commitment in the Lintel Master Services Agreement §P2.1 and published as a customer-inspectable policy on the Lintel trust page [CITE: Lintel MSA §P2.1, customer contract available on request; Lintel trust page, https://lintelstandard.com/trust — pin final URL before publish]. The commitment: when a TCB-affecting CVE is publicly disclosed, Lintel notifies affected customers within 72 hours of the CVE publication timestamp on the issuing vendor’s security bulletin (the clock starts at public disclosure, not at Lintel’s internal discovery), publishes the affected TCB SVN range, and triggers the invalidation workflow described below.

Mechanically, the protocol is what makes the earlier layers operational instead of theoretical. “Invalidate” is shorthand for four concurrent workflows, each with an observable output a procurement reviewer can check. First, the minimum-SVN policy update is pushed to the verifier trust store, raising the floor such that attestations chaining through the vulnerable SVN fail at Layer 3 verification — this is Patch or Perish applied operationally. Second, an advisory marker is appended to the SVP Transparency Log at leaves corresponding to affected SVPs, making Layer 4 a live audit surface (CVP protocol specification, SSRN 6090766, §8). Third, the affected VCEK or PCK certificate range is added to a published denylist the verifier consults alongside the manufacturer root of trust. Fourth, customers are offered re-attestation on patched firmware, re-issuing the Layer 5 commitment over a new bundle with a fresh ephemeral key bound to the current TCB SVN. Absent any of the four, invalidation is notional.

The strongest counter-view is that the 72-hour notification SLA is redundant once verifier policy mechanically rejects stale TCBs at Layer 3. If the verifier already refuses attestations under the vulnerable SVN, the written notification looks like procedural theatre — the protection is in the minimum-SVN push, not the email. The counter-view is correct about the mechanical rejection: once the minimum-SVN policy update is deployed, SVPs chaining through the old SVN fail at verification regardless of whether any buyer reads the advisory. Where it is incomplete is in the audit and procurement layer. A written 72-hour SLA gives the allocator an artifact to present to their investment committee, the regulator, or the fund auditor as evidence that the vendor’s incident response is contractual rather than best-effort. Minimum-SVN enforcement without a written notification process is a technical control; the SLA is what converts it into a procurement-inspectable one.

The procurement fit is deliberate. The AIMA Manager Due Diligence Questionnaire (2025 Edition) and the AITEC-AIMA Illustrative Questionnaire for the Due Diligence of Vendor Technology and Cyber Security (2025 Edition) both ask, under vendor incident response, for written notification SLAs and advisory histories. Absence of a stated TCB advisory protocol, from a vendor whose trust model is hardware-enclave based, is a procurement red flag — and should be.

A procurement reviewer evaluating any attested-execution vendor should ask, in writing, for the TCB advisory SLA and the last three advisory notifications the vendor has issued. If the vendor cannot produce either, the architecture behind the sales deck is a slide, not a control.
